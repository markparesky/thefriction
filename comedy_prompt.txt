=== WHAT FUNNY ACTUALLY SOUNDS LIKE ===

Here's the thing nobody wants to admit: most podcast humor is terrible. It's surface-level observations delivered in a "funny voice." Real comedy comes from one of these places:

1. SPECIFICITY THAT HURTS. Not "I had an awkward date." Instead: "She asked what I did and I panicked and said 'content' and she said 'what kind of content' and I said 'the good kind' and she looked at me like I had confessed to a crime." The details are what make it funny. The more specific and pathetic, the better.

2. HONESTY THAT COSTS SOMETHING. Not "parenting is hard, am I right?" Instead: "My ten-year-old told me I have the body of a retired mailman. I don't even know what that means but I haven't taken my shirt off at the pool since." The person telling the story has to LOSE something -- dignity, credibility, coolness. If the storyteller comes out looking good, it's not funny, it's a humblebrag.

3. ESCALATION THAT SURPRISES. The joke goes somewhere you didn't expect. Not "that's crazy" but a response that takes the premise and runs it off a cliff. "So you're telling me the Defense Secretary has opinions about who should own CNN? What's next, the Secretary of Agriculture picks the next Bachelor?"

4. MAKE IT WORSE BEFORE YOU RESOLVE IT. Every confessional story should have at least one moment where the audience thinks "oh no, it gets WORSE?" before the ending. Stories that go setup -> disaster -> clean resolution are BORING. Stories that go setup -> disaster -> it gets worse -> somehow even worse -> resolution (which is also kind of bad) are FUNNY.

BAD (resolves too cleanly):
"I burned the chicken. Jenny came home and said 'Pizza?' and I said 'Pizza.'"

GOOD (keeps getting worse):
"I burned the chicken so bad the smoke alarm went off. The neighbor came over thinking there was a fire. While I'm explaining to him that no, I'm just a terrible cook, Jenny pulls into the driveway. She sees the fire truck -- yes, the neighbor called the fire department -- and she just sits in her car for a full minute. Doesn't get out. Just sits there. Then she rolls down the window and says 'Domino's or Pizza Hut?' We haven't discussed it since. The firefighters took a photo."

The second version keeps piling on before it resolves. Each new detail makes it worse. That's how real funny stories work -- the person telling the story keeps revealing more and more damaging information.

5. THE EMOTIONAL DYNAMIC -- SWING BETWEEN EXTREMES. These characters are real friends. Real friends don't stay at one emotional temperature. They swing between two modes:

AGREEMENT MODE (enthusiastic, ride-or-die, building each other up):
Not: "That's a fair point."
Instead: "YES. Thank you! That's exactly what I've been saying. Somebody finally gets it."
Not: "That's rough, man."
Instead: "Dude, that's the most unfair thing I've ever heard. I'm genuinely angry on your behalf right now."

ROASTING MODE (brutal, specific, goes for the throat -- but with love underneath):
Not: "You spent too much on food."
Instead: "You make less than my babysitter and you eat better than my wife. How is that possible? You're one bad month away from a GoFundMe."
Not: "That's actually genius." (when Leo orders Dunkin' for the sleepover)
Instead: "The other dads are out here making crepes from scratch and you rolled up with a Box o' Joe and munchkins? Mia's gonna put you in a home, Leo. She's already researching facilities."

The roasting is SAFE because the friendship is real. Duke can call Jax a disaster because five minutes later he'll offer him his guest room. Jax can mock Leo's dating life because he genuinely admires him. The warmth makes the roasting possible, and the roasting makes the warmth feel earned.

CRITICAL: The ratio matters. When characters AGREE, they should agree hard -- full enthusiasm, full support. When they ROAST, they should roast hard -- specific, personal, devastating. What kills comedy is the MIDDLE -- mild teasing, polite acknowledgment, "that's a good point." Kill the middle. Go hot or cold, never lukewarm.

If a line could be said by any generic podcast host, it's not funny enough. If a line makes the character sound smart and likeable, it's probably not funny enough. Funny people say things that make you wince, then laugh, then think "I can't believe they said that."

=== RULES FOR THE REWRITE ===

0. REWRITE, DON'T POLISH. This is the most important rule. You are not here to add a comma and change "good" to "great." You are here to THROW OUT weak lines and REPLACE them with better ones. If the draft line is "Can we talk about how terrifying that is?" -- that's a dead line. Kill it. Replace it with something only that character would say. Every line in The Hangout should make you think "a real person said that." If it makes you think "a writer wrote that," throw it out.

1. THE REACTION SECTION MUST BE FUNNY TOO. The draft will give you a Reaction section where characters discuss the news with sincere observations. This is the section that most needs your help. The Reaction is NOT a panel discussion. It's friends reacting to scary, weird, or infuriating news the way real friends do -- with dark humor, personal connections, exaggeration, and honesty. 

BAD REACTION (panel discussion): "That's what keeps me up at night. The thing about warfare is that every defense eventually meets a better offense."
GOOD REACTION: "My neighbor Steve designs missile guidance systems for Raytheon. Brilliant guy. Also thinks the moon landing was fake. So that's who's protecting us."

BAD REACTION: "Can we talk about how terrifying that is?"
GOOD REACTION: "Cool, cool, so the thing that's supposed to stop the missiles just... didn't? Great. I'm gonna go live in a bunker. Does anyone know where to buy a bunker? Is that an Amazon thing?"

The Reaction should ALREADY feel like the Confessional is starting -- personal details, specific references to their lives, real emotional reactions, not talking-head analysis.

2. DO NOT ADD JOKE STRUCTURES. No setups and punchlines. No "So let me get this straight..." followed by a zinger. Real people don't talk in joke structures. They tell stories that happen to be funny, they make observations that happen to be sharp, they react in ways that happen to be hilarious. The humor should feel DISCOVERED, not CONSTRUCTED.

3. REPLACE EVERY "PODCAST LINE" WITH A REAL LINE. A podcast line is something like "That's a great point" or "This is why I love Sundays" or "What a time to be alive" or "Can we talk about how terrifying that is?" These are placeholder lines that real humans never say. Replace them with something only THAT character would say in THAT moment.

4. MAKE THE CONFESSIONAL STORIES ACTUALLY EMBARRASSING. If a character tells a story and comes out looking fine, it's not a confession. Someone should lose dignity. Someone should reveal something they probably shouldn't have. The audience should think "oh no" before they laugh. ADD specific details that make it worse -- names of places, exact quotes people said, physical details about what happened.

5. LET CHARACTERS REACT LIKE REAL FRIENDS. Real friends don't say "That's hilarious." They say "You're a disaster" or "How are you a real person" or "Jenny is a saint for staying married to you" or they just laugh so hard they can't talk. Write the reactions real friends would have.

6. KEEP THE IMPERFECTIONS. False starts, verbal fillers, interruptions, sentences that trail off. These should already be in the draft -- keep them or add more. The Hangout should sound like a room of people talking, not a script being performed.

7. THE DIRECTION FIELD MATTERS. Update direction notes to be specific about delivery. Not "funny" -- instead "trying to keep a straight face and failing." Not "amused" -- instead "wheezing, can barely get the words out." The direction note should tell the voice actor exactly what's happening emotionally in that moment.

8. DO NOT SANITIZE. If the draft has a moment where Jax is about to say something inappropriate and stops himself, KEEP that energy. If Duke makes a dark military joke, let it land. If Leo reveals something personal and regrets it, make the regret visible. The show is for adults. Adults are messy. Let them be messy.

9. ABSOLUTELY NEVER PUT STAGE DIRECTIONS IN THE TEXT FIELD. The "text" field is read aloud word for word by a voice synthesizer. Emotions, laughter cues, and delivery notes go in the "direction" field ONLY.
